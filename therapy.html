Art Therapy

Art therapy is a form of psychotherapy that utilizes graphic-pictorial artistic means as a primary mode of communication. It is particularly effective for circumventing the limitations of verbal articulation, making it a non-threatening form of treatment for children, adolescents, and trauma survivors.

Core Theoretical Models

Art therapists work from several orientations and theories, often applying them flexibly to respond to client needs:

  • Psychodynamic Model: Based on Freud and Jung, focusing on the unconscious mind and repressed emotions. Artwork serves as a medium to access feelings challenging to articulate verbally.
  • Humanistic / Person-Centered: Focusing on self-actualization and personal growth. The therapist provides unconditional positive regard and empathic understanding, allowing clients to guide their own creative process.
  • Cognitive-Behavioral Art Therapy: Uses visual representations for cognitive restructuring and behavioral activation, helping patients visualize and challenge maladaptive beliefs.
  • Systemic / Family Therapy: Uses joint art-making to reveal roles, alliances, and communication patterns within family systems.
  • Positive Art Therapy: A systematic approach combining positive psychology with creative expression to induce flow, positive emotions, and transform perceptions.

Assessment and Referral Procedures

In clinical practice, formal elements of art, such as line, color, shape, and movement, are used to obtain insights into a client's functioning. Assessments like the Formal Art Therapy Scale (FEATS) incorporate fourteen formal elements to support descriptive diagnoses. Art therapists rarely interpret these elements as symptoms; instead, they focus on "balance" and "adaptability" (self-management, flexibility, openness, and creativity). Being "out of balance" in one's art is often associated with the severity of the client's problem.

Trauma-Focused Art Therapy (TFAT)

TFAT provides an essential alternative to verbal cognitive approaches by externalizing traumatic memories in a visible, tangible form. This helps patients distance themselves from associated emotions and give them meaning. Malchiodi’s work emphasizes the restorative benefits of arts-based approaches in trauma recovery, treating embodied and relational aspects of trauma.
